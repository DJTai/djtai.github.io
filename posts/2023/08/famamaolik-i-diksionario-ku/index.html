Have you ever looked for a word in “The Official CHAMORRO-ENGLISH Dictionary”? If you have, perhaps you’ve noticed that the words aren’t sorted exactly according to i atfabetun Chamoru. Take for instance the following set of words pulled directly from the dictionary mentioned above

To be honest though, it’s not a huge problem. It’s easy to handle in real-time and, so long as the word exists in the dictionary, I tend to find what I’m looking for.

However…what if I wanted the words to be truly sorted?

The results are interesting but also expected - at least for Python. While this isn’t the exact same sorting as used in the blue dictionary, it does point out a problem: we can’t rely on “standard” sorting procedures when working with Chamorro!

I’ve been (slowly, but surely) working on fine-tuning my dictionary and have been doing everything in Python. For me, the issue arose due to how Python’s sorted() function works: it uses the character’s Unicode decimal value2.

This is precisely why I created my own sorting algorithm. Let’s look at it in action:

In [2]: chamoru_words = [

In [3]: sorted(chamoru_words, key=chamoru_sort)

Success! Biba! Some things I want to point out are:

  • Vowels and their accented counterparts hold the same value (e.g., a and 치 will get sorted equally)
  • Casing does not affect order
  • n comes before 침 which comes before ng. I may have to revist this one, primarily because I’m not 100% confident that all of the words in my dictionary are spelled “correctly” (e.g. I may have mangaigi instead of manggaigi)

I feel like I still have some work to do - it’s not as straight forward as I thought it would be! For instance: while they don’t adhere to i atfabetun Chamoru, what happens with family names? While perhaps not standard, I think it makes sense to have names like Aquiningoc and Quitugua appear in a dictionary - especially since we can pull out the Chamorro words they’re derived from!
