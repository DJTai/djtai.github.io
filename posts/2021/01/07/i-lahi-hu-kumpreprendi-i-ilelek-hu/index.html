I Lahi-hu Kumpreprendi i Ilelek-hu

My Son Understands What I'm Saying

By David Taitingfong | January 07, 2021 | 2 minutes
todu chamoru

Kada diha hu usa fino’ Chamoru gi i gima’-hu yan i familiå-ku, i asaguå-hu yan i lahi-hu, si Lorie yan si Leo. Gi minagåhet, ti sessu hu usa nuebu na palåbra siha yan siha, lao siempri hu adingani siha gi fino’ Chamoru. Guaha na biåhi na hu ke’usa nuebu na palåbra siha, piót yan i lahi-hu, ya kada diha siña manli’i’ yu’ na ha na’la’tungu’ gui’. Hu tungu’ na ha kumprendi annai hu sangåni gui':

  1. Maila’ mågi
  2. Nihi ta tulaika (i magågu-mu / i pañåles-mu)
  3. Huchum i petta fan
  4. Mungnga (mamacha, pumacha, kumassas, tumångis, etc.)
  5. Chiku fan
  6. Kao ñålang hao?

Banidosu yu’ nu guiya, ya gi minagåhet banidosu yu’ lokkui’ nu guahu na siña hu adingani yan i familiå-ku gi fino’ Chamoru, achokka’ adumididi'.

Todu ha’ para på’gu. Bula guinaiya! — DJT.

English Translation

Everyday I use Chamoru in my house with my family, my wife and my son, Lorie and Leo. Honestly, I don’t often use new words with them, but I certainly talk to them in Chamoru. Sometimes I try to use new words, especially with my son, and I can see that everyday he learns a bit more. I know that he can understand when I say:

  1. Come here
  2. Lets change (your clothes / your diaper)
  3. Close the door please
  4. Don’t (touch anything, touch that, scratch, cry, etc.)
  5. Kiss please
  6. Are you hungry?

I’m proud of him, and honestly I’m proud of myself too that I can converse with my family in Chamoru, even if just a little.

That’s it for now. Much love! — DJT
